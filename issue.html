Many students, especially those from low-income or underrepresented backgrounds, lack access to quality STEM education. This gap limits their opportunities to explore, develop, and excel in critical fields like science, technology, engineering, and math, creating disparities in future careers and innovation potential.

STEM Skills Are in High Demand

Across Georgia and the nation, careers in science, technology, engineering, and mathematics (STEM) are growing at a faster rate than most other jobs. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, STEM occupations are projected to grow by over 10% by 2033, with median wages exceeding $100,000—far above the national average [source].

In Georgia alone, roles like mechanical engineers, software developers, web developers, and electronics engineering technicians are classified as “hot jobs,” meeting three key criteria:

  • Above-average job growth
  • Above-average wages
  • At least 100 openings per year

[source]

Yet Too Many Georgia Students Are Falling Behind

Despite this booming opportunity, far too few Georgia students are on track to seize it. In 2024, only 27% of 5th grade students achieved science proficiency on the Georgia Milestones assessment. This represents a critical gap—not just in learning, but in opportunity [source].

And this problem doesn’t only impact students—it affects the future of Georgia’s economy, workforce competitiveness, and technological innovation.

Most Learning Happens Outside School—We’re Wasting That Time

Students spend more than 80% of their waking hours outside of school during the year. Afterschool hours are an untapped resource where kids can explore, build, tinker, and learn on their own terms [source].

That’s where afterschool STEM comes in.

Afterschool STEM Works—And Students Thrive

Research from Harvard’s PEAR Institute and others shows that more than 70% of students in high-quality afterschool STEM programs report gains in:

  • STEM interest and identity
  • STEM career awareness
  • Critical thinking and perseverance

These aren’t just fun activities—they’re proven pathways to greater confidence, improved academic outcomes, and long-term engagement in STEM fields [source].

For example:

  • A Chicago afterschool “Science Club” program increased STEM college major enrollment from 1% to 32% of participants [source].
  • The Clubhouse Network helped 80% of alumni pursue careers they attributed directly to their afterschool STEM experiences [source].

We Must Close the Equity Gap

The wealthiest 20% of families spend 7x more on out-of-school enrichment than the poorest 20%. Without access to quality programs, countless students—especially in underserved communities—are locked out of the innovation economy before they even get started [source].
